What I am good at...

Problem identification & Requirements engineering

Identifying problems early on, and effectively is essential to organisational success. All too often problems are identified and only their symptoms treated. Or worse, the problem is mis-identified resulting in a waste of valuable time and resources with no beneficial outcome.

Requirements engineering is a term used in academia to describe not only the identification of the problem, but the derivation of effective project specifications that resolve the problem.

My previous experience includes working as a APM certified project manager and business analyst on a £7m ERP upgrade project. This area has formed to core of my PhD, providing an extensive academic understanding of how individual and teams identify and make sense of problems.

Distributed work strategy

Distributed working describes how a team operates when the team members work from multiple locations. It affords a valuable alternative to traditional co-located office-based working. Organisations can benefit from reduced overheads as teams no longer require valuable office space. Employees meanwhile benefit from a greater work-life balance.

There are many forms of distributed working, from fully distributed teams that rarely meet, to teams who are free to work from home multiple days a week but still maintain a limited office presence. Together we can derive the best strategy for adopting and managing distributed work in your organisation.

My PhD research explores whether distributed teams identify and resolve problems differently to traditional co-located teams, and whether these differences have any impact on performance.

My academic hat

- the focus of my PhD

My PhD research aim is to prompt discussion about the need for traditional office work. Is it instead possible for organisations to have teams that work from home and who still tackle complicated projects? Are such teams capable of complex socio-cognitive tasks like designing that require both interaction and creativity and that are typically complicated in co-located design studios.

For my research I have run an extensive series of experiments that compare the performance of local/co-located and remote/distributed teams as they address a number of collaborative creativity activities. These activities are both abstract and complex requiring teams to treat them as problems that require understanding and resolving. I study how these teams think and work together to make sense of the activities as well as differences in their creative performance.

My research question...

Are teams that work from home...

...as creative...

...as traditional, office-based teams?

(and therefore as good at identifying and resolving problems)
